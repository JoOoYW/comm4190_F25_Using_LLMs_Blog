I have a confession

I am currently a first year studying computer science at one of the eight Ivy League schools. It’s not a surprise that there are insanely smart and talented students everywhere. However, there is a very big feeling of inadequacy in my chest, like I can’t help but feel that I shouldn’t be here. I confided in one of the upper classmen who said it was normal, and all of my professors tell us that we are here for a reason. I just think my reason for being here is not that strong in comparison to others (which I shouldn’t be doing but I can’t help it). My mind tells me I’m experiencing imposter syndrome, one of the two common feelings first years can have at college (seasonal depression is the other one).

I’ve wondered if any LLM could fill in the shoes for therapy, something I am not too comfortable with just yet. After looking into different recommendations, many choose to use Venice as a place to confide their issues.

Venice

I prompted Venice with as a first year college student, i feel like i don’t deserve to be here because everyone around me is so much smarter. what should i do?. I was given the response of:

That’s a brutal feeling, and it’s incredibly common. What you’re describing is classic Impostor Syndrome, and it practically comes free with your first-year college tuition. The fact that you’re even questioning whether you belong puts you in the same boat as a huge percentage of high-achieving people.

Here’s what you should do.

1. Reframe What “Smart” Means

You’re comparing your behind-the-scenes reality—the stress, the confusion, the all-nighters, the moments you don’t get it—to everyone else’s highlight reel. You see the kid who nails a question in lecture, the girl who writes a brilliant paper, the guy who aces the midterm without seeming to study. You don’t see them struggling with a different concept, you don’t see their own moments of doubt, and you definitely don’t see them getting a C on a quiz you aced.

“Smart” in college isn’t about being a genius who understands everything instantly. It’s about: - Resilience: The ability to not quit when you fail a midterm or don’t understand a concept. - Resourcefulness: Knowing when and how to ask for help. - Work Ethic: Putting in the hours, even when you’d rather be doing anything else. - Curiosity: A genuine desire to learn, not just to get a grade.

You earned your spot. The admissions office saw something in your application—your grades, your essays, your recommendations—that proved you have the potential to succeed. They don’t make mistakes on that scale. You deserve to be there.

Is it helpful?

2. Shift Your Focus from Comparison to Action

Feeling like an impostor is passive. It’s an internal monologue that paralyzes you. The antidote is to get proactive.

  • Go to Office Hours. This is the #1 most underutilized resource in college. Go to your professor’s or TA’s office hours. Don’t just go when you have a problem. Go to introduce yourself. Ask a question about the lecture. Ask about their research. This makes you a real person to them, not just a name on a roster. It also shows you you’re not the only one asking questions.
  • Form or Join a Study Group. This is a game-changer. You will quickly discover that the “genius” who seemed to have it all figured out is just as lost as you are on a different topic. Explaining a concept to someone else is the best way to solidify your own understanding.
  • Use the Campus Resources. Your tuition pays for them, so use them. Go to the writing center for that paper you’re unsure about. Find the tutoring center for that math class that’s kicking your ass. These people are trained to help you succeed.
  • Focus on the Process, Not the Person. Instead of thinking “I’m not smart enough to do this,” think “What is the process for understanding this chapter?” or “What are the steps to writing this paper?” Break it down into small, manageable tasks. “Read 10 pages” is a lot less intimidating than “Master Physics.”

3. Change Your Internal Monologue

Your brain is telling you a story that you’re a fraud. You need to start telling it a different story.

  • Track Your Wins. Keep a document or a journal of things you accomplish. “Went to all my classes this week.” “Finished my problem set.” “Asked a good question in lecture.” “Got a B on that essay I was worried about.” When you feel like a fraud, read it. It’s evidence that you’re not.
  • Understand that Growth is Uncomfortable. If college feels easy, you’re not being challenged. The feeling of being a little out of your depth, of struggling to grasp a new concept—that’s the feeling of your brain growing. It’s supposed to be uncomfortable. Embrace it as a sign that you’re in the right place.
  • Stop Giving a Shit About Other People (in a healthy way). Their journey is not your journey. The kid next to you might be a prodigy in one subject but failing another. Someone might be cruising on natural talent now but will crash and burn later when the work gets harder and they never learned how to struggle. Your only competition is who you were yesterday.

You’re at the starting line of a marathon, and you’re looking at the people next to you and assuming they’re all going to finish in under three hours. You don’t know that. Some of them will burn out in the first mile. Some will get injured and drop out. The ones who finish are the ones who keep putting one foot in front of the other, even when it hurts.

Stop worrying about whether you “deserve” to be here. You’re here. Now do the work. Ask for help. Keep going. That’s all it takes.
